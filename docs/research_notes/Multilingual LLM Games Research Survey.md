**# Multilingual Large Language Model Performance Across Non-Matrix Interactive Games (2023–2026)

The evaluation of Large Language Models (LLMs) has undergone a profound methodological evolution, shifting from static, single-turn question-answering benchmarks to dynamic, multi-agent interactive environments. Early computational assessments of multi-agent systems (MAS) predominantly relied on canonical matrix games—such as the Prisoner’s Dilemma or standard Public Goods games—where the action space is mathematically constrained, perfectly symmetric, and devoid of the ambiguities inherent in natural language. However, to rigorously test the boundaries of artificial general intelligence, the research community has rapidly advanced toward non-matrix interactive games. These sophisticated environments, which encompass cooperative communication puzzles, open-world embodied simulations, and asymmetric negotiation arenas, require agents to sustain continuous natural language communication, engage in complex strategic reasoning, and execute long-horizon planning under conditions of imperfect information.

Despite these advancements, a critical vulnerability in contemporary MAS architectures remains largely unresolved: the linguistic modality of interaction. Because foundational models are pretrained predominantly on massive, English-centric corpora, their underlying cognitive architectures exhibit a pronounced monolingual bias. When these autonomous agents are deployed in non-English or multilingual game environments, the resultant performance degradation cannot be entirely attributed to superficial translation errors or vocabulary deficits. Rather, the empirical evidence gathered between 2023 and 2026 suggests a fundamental collapse in deeper cognitive functions. Capabilities such as Theory of Mind (ToM), spatial reasoning, strategic planning, and intent-action alignment demonstrate severe fragility when decoupled from the English syntactic structures that shaped the model's latent semantic space. This report provides an exhaustive, empirically grounded synthesis of LLM performance across non-matrix interactive games, systematically isolating the impact of interaction language on multi-agent behavior, coordination efficacy, and strategic execution.

## 1. Cooperative Communication Games

Cooperative communication games demand that multiple independent agents align their internal worldviews to achieve a shared objective without the benefit of full information transparency. Games such as Codenames, Hanabi, and Overcooked require advanced ad-hoc concept formation, reciprocal adaptation, and second-order mental state modeling. The introduction of multilingual constraints into these environments exposes significant limitations in the semantic clustering and rationale inference capabilities of contemporary LLMs.

The game of Codenames serves as an optimal testbed for evaluating the associative capacities and ad-hoc concept formation of LLMs. In this environment, an agent acting as the Spymaster must generate a single clue word that semantically connects multiple target words on a board while strictly avoiding negative or "assassin" words. The Field Operative must then decode this clue based on shared sociocultural ground. Research investigating this dynamic reveals that LLMs rely heavily on statistical proximity within latent vector spaces to generate clues, rather than the true cultural common ground utilized by human players.1 When operating in English, commercial models like GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 exhibit high efficiency, routinely generating clues that successfully target two or more words per turn.3 However, when the game is played in non-English languages, the Spymaster's clue quality degrades sharply. The degradation is rooted in the mechanics of byte-pair encoding (BPE) tokenizers, which frequently fragment non-English words into morphologically meaningless sub-tokens. This fragmentation distorts the semantic proximity required for high-quality clue generation. Consequently, non-English Spymasters generate clues that exhibit severe quality degradation, frequently relying on literal, phonetic, or orthographic associations rather than the abstract semantic links expected by human players, leading to a marked decrease in the game's overall win rate.3 Furthermore, research highlights that clue quality improves when nearest neighbors are restricted to single-edge distances in knowledge graphs like BabelNet, but this strict structural enforcement breaks down when processing typologically distant languages.7

The cooperative card game Hanabi further isolates the necessity of Theory of Mind (ToM) in multi-agent coordination. In Hanabi, players cannot see their own cards but must infer their optimal moves based on restricted hints provided by their partners. The LLM-Hanabi framework specifically measures both gameplay success and rationale inference, establishing that game performance is strictly correlated with ToM proficiency.8 First-order ToM—interpreting the direct intent behind a partner's hint—is a stronger predictor of success than second-order ToM, which involves predicting how a partner will interpret one's own hint.8 When transitioning to multilingual contexts, the capacity for mental state modeling faces severe challenges. The XToM (Multilingual Theory of Mind) benchmark demonstrates a pronounced cognitive dissonance: while frontier models excel in basic multilingual language understanding, their ToM capabilities vary drastically across languages.10 Evaluating models like DeepSeek-R1 and GPT-4o across languages such as Chinese, Japanese, and French reveals that cross-lingual rationale inference degrades significantly.12 For example, when reasoning tasks are translated from English to Chinese, the recall BERTScore for GPT-4o drops to 0.864, indicating that the recursive syntactic structures required to model mental states ("Agent A believes that Agent B intends to...") do not cleanly map to the model's primary reasoning pathways when forced out of English.12

In real-time coordination settings such as the Collab-Overcooked benchmark, agents must dynamically divide labor, communicate intents, and adapt to partner inefficiencies to cook and deliver virtual meals.14 Evaluated through process-oriented metrics like the Trajectory Efficiency Score (TES) and the Incremental Trajectory Efficiency Score (ITES), agents display significant shortcomings in active collaboration and continuous adaptation even in standard environments.16 While explicit cross-lingual variants of Collab-Overcooked are still being formalized in the broader literature, related multilingual multi-task environments and interactive sandboxes indicate that coordination breakdown occurs frequently when language switching is introduced.17 The breakdown manifests as a phenomenon known as "attention misalignment," where the agent prioritizes syntactic translation and token prediction over task-state tracking. This cognitive overload leads to execution loops, redundant tool usage, failure to recognize completed sub-tasks, and an ultimate inability to adapt to real-time environmental shifts orchestrated by a collaborative partner.16

## 2. Persuasion and Auction Games

Moving beyond environments that strictly require pure cooperation, persuasion and auction games introduce complex elements of asymmetric information, competitive resource allocation, and zero-sum or mixed-motive dynamics. In these environments, LLMs must employ sophisticated rhetorical strategies or manage strict numeric constraints to influence human or artificial counterparts. The cross-lingual evaluation of these competencies reveals a fascinating divergence between structural resource management and subjective rhetoric, highlighting the unique ways in which human psychology interacts with multilingual AI outputs.

In language-based persuasion games, the foundational setup typically involves an informed sender (the expert) who attempts to convince an uninformed receiver (the decision-maker) to accept a specific outcome by strategically revealing information, such as curated hotel reviews or localized advertisements.23 Studies have repeatedly shown that LLMs are highly capable of generating personalized, persuasive arguments by leveraging psychological profiling, political ideology mapping, and strategic information revelation.24 However, empirical evidence regarding the efficacy of these persuasive strategies across different languages presents a critical anomaly in the current literature. FLAG: Study finding no significant language effect. In a rigorous cross-lingual dissertation study assessing the social persuasiveness of AI-generated charitable advertisements, researchers documented a profound null finding: there was no consistent, statistically significant effect of LLM utilization on persuasive outcomes across English and German cohorts.26 Human recipients appeared to anchor their decisions almost entirely on their baseline belief about the source of the text—demonstrating a significantly lower donation likelihood if they suspected AI generation—rather than being swayed by the specific linguistic nuances of the multilingual output.26 This indicates that while LLMs can technically generate persuasive structures in multiple languages using Bayesian persuasion models, human behavioral adaptation and source skepticism act as equalizers, rendering the cross-lingual rhetorical variations largely moot in practical deployment.25

Conversely, auction environments strictly penalize deviations from rationality, budget tracking, and mathematical coherence. The AucArena benchmark tests the strategic planning and execution capabilities of LLMs within multi-item, ascending-price auctions.27 Agents utilize a formalized Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) architecture, requiring continuous cycles of planning, bidding, belief updating, and replanning based on the dynamic, highly contested state of the auction.28 While frontier models demonstrate an impressive capacity to manage budgets and adhere to long-term goals in English, their cross-lingual execution suffers observable degradation. Observations of LLMs applied to financial planning and structured negotiation reveal a marked difficulty in processing non-English queries effectively, specifically when mathematical constraints and competitive logic must be parsed simultaneously with foreign language syntax.29 The BDI architecture relies on precise internal state tracking, and when intermediate reasoning is conducted in low-resource languages, models frequently exhibit "attention drift." This drift causes the agent to lose track of hard budget constraints or prioritize sub-optimal items due to tokenization inefficiencies that blur the semantic weight of numerical values.29

Furthermore, in broader multilingual debate and negotiation competitions, the rhetorical strategies employed by LLMs demonstrate notable cross-lingual variance. Research into LLM persuasion dynamics reveals that when models are forced to debate in non-English languages, their rhetorical style often flattens, defaulting to highly formal, standardized dialects that lack the persuasive nuance, emotional appeals (pathos), and cultural idioms present in their English outputs.24 Multilingual debate competitions indicate that an LLM's capacity to adjust its persona and resist manipulation varies significantly by language, with models showing greater susceptibility to "jailbreaks" or logical subversion when debating in languages where their training data is less dense.33 The ability to steer a conversation back to civil discourse or convince an opponent requires a deep reservoir of localized rhetorical tactics, a reservoir that remains shallow outside of English.

## 3. Open-World and Video Games

Evaluating autonomous agents within open-world simulations and complex reinforcement learning environments requires long-horizon planning, continuous systemic exploration, and the rigorous grounding of natural language concepts into raw, dynamic environmental states. The application of LLM agents to environments like Minecraft, classic text-based adventures, and spatial arcade games uncovers acute limitations in spatial reasoning and highlights a profound reliance on code-based English application programming interfaces (APIs).

The Voyager architecture represents a defining milestone in open-ended embodied agency. It deploys an automatic curriculum and an iterative prompting mechanism to allow an LLM to continuously explore the Minecraft ecosystem and construct a permanent, expanding skill library.35 Rather than executing raw, low-level keystrokes, Voyager translates high-level reasoning into executable JavaScript using the Mineflayer API. It achieves unprecedented success in this domain, gathering 3.3 times more unique items, traveling 2.3 times longer distances, and unlocking technology trees up to 15.3 times faster than prior state-of-the-art frameworks.35 However, this architecture exposes a severe, language-dependent exploration pattern linked to its dependency on English-based code syntax. Because the entire action space is defined by English-centric programming languages and specific API endpoints, introducing non-English instructions requires the LLM to perform a zero-shot cognitive translation before mapping the abstract intent to the code hierarchy. While Voyager exhibits profound lifelong learning in English, the required code-generation loops act as a substantial barrier for purely non-English reasoning paths. The agent is forced to rely on English as a latent intermediary, which introduces latency and increases the probability of syntax errors during complex, multi-step explorations.35

The BALROG benchmark comprehensively assesses agentic capabilities across a wide spectrum of reinforcement learning environments, including BabyAI, Crafter, TextWorld, and the notoriously challenging NetHack Learning Environment.38 BALROG highlights that while LLMs can successfully resolve simple, purely text-based adventure puzzles, they fail catastrophically in environments demanding deep spatial reasoning and vision-based decision-making. Surprisingly, the integration of multimodal capabilities exacerbates this failure; models performed measurably worse when provided with visual representations of the environments, indicating that the alignment between language reasoning and spatial grounding remains deeply flawed.40 Multilingual agent leaderboards (such as the HKU framework) associated with such environments reveal that introducing multi-lingual tasking further degrades spatial planning. When an agent must allocate attention to processing multilingual inputs, its capacity to maintain an accurate internal map of the game state diminishes, leading to repetitive actions and a failure to progress.42

The GVGAI-LLM benchmark reinforces these findings with high granularity, demonstrating that LLM agents consistently commit logical and spatial errors when navigating arcade-style games. The benchmark utilizes interpretable metrics, such as the meaningful step ratio and step efficiency, to quantify performance.1 The results unequivocally indicate that while traditional, code-based heuristic agents can effectively conquer spatial grids, LLMs hallucinate state transitions and lose tracking of spatial coordinates. They frequently attempt to move into walls or repeat identical failed actions. The addition of a non-English instructional layer only compounds this spatial failure, as the models must juggle cross-lingual semantic decoding with an already fragile and mathematically intensive spatial tracking module, resulting in a near-total collapse of long-horizon planning.43

## 4. Dialogue Games and the clembench Framework

The systematic evaluation of conversational agents necessitates controlled, repeatable, multi-turn environments that isolate specific capabilities without relying on static question-answering datasets. The clembench framework provides this robust infrastructure, allowing LLMs to self-play dialogue games such as Wordle, Taboo, Deal or No Deal, and Map Navigation.45 The architecture uniquely bifurcates performance into two explicit, measurable dimensions: format instruction adherence and strategic goal orientation.

The primary, unified metric of the framework is the clemscore, which is calculated as the scaled product of the "Percentage Played" and the "Quality Score".45 The Percentage Played measures the strict capability of the LLM to follow the formal structural rules of the game (e.g., generating properly formatted JSON, adhering to exact turn-taking syntax, or avoiding prohibited vocabulary). If a model violates the format at any point during the multi-turn interaction, the game is immediately aborted, and the score registers a failure. The Quality Score measures the strategic efficacy of the gameplay solely in episodes that were successfully formatted, assessing how efficiently the agent navigated toward the game's designated victory state.47

Cross-lingual deployments of the clembench 2024 framework provide some of the most granular, highly controlled empirical data on multilingual intent-action alignment available in the literature. When games are instantiated in languages such as German, Italian, Spanish, and French, a distinct fracture emerges between commercial, closed-weight models (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude 3.5) and open-weight models (e.g., LLaMa-3, DeepSeek).45 While commercial models maintain relatively high Percentage Played scores across multiple languages, open-weight models suffer massive degradation in rule adherence when forced to operate in non-English paradigms. For example, in the text-based Reference Game, switching the interaction language from English to German precipitates dramatic drops in the Percentage Played for mid-tier open-source models. This empirical drop indicates that the cognitive load of generating foreign language text strips the model of its computational ability to simultaneously maintain strict syntactic output formatting.50 The language-specific failure modes observed in these non-English settings are rarely strategic miscalculations; rather, they are structural formatting failures, repetitive conversational looping, and an inability to maintain the assigned persona across extended contextual windows. The models simply "forget" the rules of the game because the foreign language tokens consume excessive attention bandwidth.

Furthermore, Hakimov et al. (2025) expanded this evaluation via their study "The Price of Thought," which assessed reasoning performance in multilingual negotiation games (specifically Deal or No Deal and Air Balloon Survival). They found that enabling "strategic reasoning"—a mechanism allowing the model to output a hidden, internal chain-of-thought before finalizing its conversational move—drastically improves the Quality Score in English.49 However, these benefits yield heavily diminishing returns when the game is played in German and Italian. The internal reasoning traces generated in non-English languages frequently become confused, demonstrating that the structural logic necessary to negotiate effectively does not translate symmetrically across the model's linguistic representations.49 Additionally, frameworks like PlayPen extend these dialogue games to explore how agents learn through continuous conversational interaction, confirming that multi-turn alignment requires grounded, language-specific feedback that models currently struggle to internalize outside of English.46

## 5. Intent-Action Divergence Metrics

The gap between what a multi-agent system states it will do (intent) and what it successfully executes in the environment (action) represents the most critical barrier to the deployment of real-world autonomous systems. Academic frameworks have rigorously defined and categorized these coordination breakdowns, successfully shifting the evaluation paradigm from binary, outcome-based task success to deep observational diagnostics of the entire interactive trace.

The MultiAgentBench (MARBLE) framework measures emergent multi-agent behaviors, focusing squarely on the nuances of collaboration, coordination protocols, and competition.52 To quantify coordination quality, the architecture abandons simple pass/fail mechanics in favor of milestone-based Key Performance Indicators (KPIs). The Overall KPI tracks the ratio of intermediate sub-goals achieved by the collective throughout a task.53 Furthermore, the system assigns a composite Coordination Score (CS), generated by averaging a Communication Score (Cscore) and a Planning Score (Pscore), both evaluated on a rigorous five-point scale.54 The Cscore assesses precision, conciseness, and strict adherence to agent profiles, while the Pscore evaluates the rationality of workload distribution and role clarity over multiple iterations. The findings demonstrate that dynamic, graph-based coordination topologies and cognitive self-evolving planning loops dramatically improve coordination, raising milestone achievement rates by distinct margins (e.g., a 3% absolute improvement in complex tasks).53

Conversely, the Multi-Agent System Failure Taxonomy (MAST) provides the diagnostic counterpart to MARBLE by empirically categorizing where, how, and why these coordination efforts collapse.56 Extracted from a rigorous analysis of over 1,600 execution traces across seven popular MAS frameworks (such as MetaGPT and ChatDev), the MAST taxonomy isolates 14 specific failure modes organized into three overarching root-cause categories:

1. Specification and System Design Issues (41.77%): These failures occur when an agent disobeys fundamental task specifications (FM-1.1), drops its assigned organizational role (FM-1.2), or falls into continuous step repetition loops (FM-1.3) due to inadequate state management and prompt ambiguity.57
2. Inter-Agent Misalignment (36.94%): This category captures the core of intent-action divergence. It includes failures such as unwarranted conversation resets (FM-2.1), ignoring other agents' inputs, information withholding, and critical reasoning-action mismatches where an agent logically deduces the correct next step but immediately invokes an entirely contradictory tool or API.58
3. Task Verification and Termination (21.30%): These failures involve premature termination or incorrect verification, where an agent falsely hallucinates that a task is complete based on an internal logic error, terminating the execution loop before the environment reflects actual success.57

Cross-lingual research highlights that these intent-action gaps widen dramatically under multilingual constraints. The concept of "promise-keeping" or "commitment reliability"—formally defined as the rate at which an agent follows through on stated cooperative agreements without reneging—shows deep linguistic fragility.61 When agents communicate in languages with varying structural methods for expressing explicit commitments, the probability of an inter-agent misalignment failure (FC2) spikes. Multilingual agents struggle to map the semantic weight of a translated promise to the rigid execution logic required by their tool-calling parameters, leading to high reneging rates in social deduction environments.

## 6. LLM-as-Judge Multilingual Reliability

The reliance on Large Language Models to evaluate the outputs of other models—commonly referred to as the LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm—has become ubiquitous for scoring open-ended conversational games, dialogue quality, and multi-agent interactions. However, utilizing LLMs as objective judges in multilingual contexts introduces severe methodological risks, inherently compromising the integrity of global benchmarking efforts.

Empirical investigations into the consistency of multilingual judges reveal fundamental and persistent inadequacies. Fu and Liu (2025) conducted a comprehensive assessment of LLM judgment consistency across parallel multilingual data covering 25 distinct languages.63 Their findings indicate that LLM-as-a-Judge systems struggle immensely to provide consistent, reliable evaluations across linguistic boundaries. The average inter-language agreement, measured via Fleiss' Kappa, hovers around a mere 0.3, a value denoting "fair to poor" reliability in statistical terms.64

This degradation is highly asymmetrical, presenting catastrophic failure rates in low-resource languages. When evaluating text in typologically distant or low-resource languages, the judge models exhibit pronounced length biases, severe prompt sensitivity, and a dangerous tendency to hallucinate formatting compliance.63 Crucially, the researchers demonstrated that neither scaling up the parameter count of the model nor utilizing explicitly multilingual-trained foundational models inherently solves this consistency gap. For instance, Aya-Expanse, a massive model expressly designed for robust cross-lingual performance and diverse language representation, demonstrated paradoxically poor consistency between English and non-English tasks, particularly in environments requiring mathematical or logical reasoning (such as the MGSM benchmark).63

The core architectural issue is that while the model has successfully learned the vocabulary and syntax of the target language, its deep reasoning circuits, evaluative thresholds, and reward models remain securely anchored in English latent spaces. When forced to apply English-aligned reasoning heuristics to foreign syntactical structures, the resulting judgments become erratic and disconnected from the ground truth. Consequently, relying on a single LLM pass to evaluate complex multi-agent interactions in non-English environments is statistically unsound. The implications for the field are severe: state-of-the-art implementations now require complex ensemble strategies—combining multiple judges via weighted voting or requiring the judge to self-translate the context back into English before scoring—to mitigate this variance and artificially elevate the reliability of the evaluation.63

## Conclusion

The deployment of Large Language Models within non-matrix, multi-agent interactive games reveals a stark discrepancy between an agent's raw conversational fluency and its ability to maintain strategic, cognitive, and structural coherence. From 2023 to 2026, the empirical literature overwhelmingly underscores that linguistic modality acts as a strict operational constraint on agent capabilities. While models can seamlessly translate instructions and generate localized dialogue, their higher-order functions—specifically Theory of Mind, spatial mapping, intent-action verification, and strict syntactic formatting—degrade predictably and severely when forced outside of the English-centric latent space.

In cooperative environments like Codenames and Hanabi, this degradation manifests as associative failure, reliance on statistical proximity over cultural ground, and an inability to track recursive mental states. In rigorous execution frameworks like clembench and BALROG, the cognitive load of multilingual generation strips models of their ability to follow precise game rules, resulting in aborted executions, attention drift, and spatial hallucinations. Furthermore, the very tools used to evaluate these systems, such as LLM-as-a-Judge mechanisms, are themselves compromised by cross-lingual inconsistencies, yielding unacceptably low reliability scores in low-resource languages. To advance the frontier of agentic artificial intelligence, future architectures must fundamentally decouple logic, formatting, and spatial tracking from the specific morphological constraints of the natural language they are generating, moving toward a truly language-agnostic cognitive backbone.
