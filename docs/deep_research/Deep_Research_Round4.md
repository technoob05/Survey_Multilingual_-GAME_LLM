# Advanced Methodologies in Multilingual Large Language Models: A Comprehensive Analysis of Steering Asymmetries, Strategic Calibration, Evaluative Reliability, and Cognitive Emulation

## The Metacognitive and Architectural Landscape of Multilingual AI

The rapid globalization of Large Language Models (LLMs) has necessitated a profound reevaluation of their underlying architectural, behavioral, and cognitive mechanics. As the deployment of these artificial intelligence systems scales across diverse linguistic demographics, researchers have increasingly observed that the functional capabilities of LLMs are not language-agnostic. Instead, foundational models exhibit severe operational disparities when transitioning from their dominant pre-training language—predominantly English—into non-English and low-resource linguistic environments. This comprehensive analytical report investigates the functional significance of these linguistic asymmetries across four critical, highly specialized domains of contemporary computational linguistics and AI alignment.

First, the report examines the scaling asymmetries inherent in inference-time activation steering, specifically utilizing the Activation Addition (ActAdd) methodology, to understand how non-English latent representations necessitate fundamentally different geometric interventions compared to English representations. Second, the analysis transitions into the realm of multi-agent game-theoretic environments, evaluating the calibration, reliability, and Expected Calibration Error (ECE) of strategy classifiers mapping LLM behaviors in social dilemmas such as the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma (IPD). Third, the report provides an empirical validation of ensemble judging protocols—evaluating the cost-reliability trade-offs, inter-rater consistency metrics, and failure coupling rates of the "LLM-as-a-Judge" paradigm when forced to operate across diverse linguistic boundaries. Finally, the analysis explores the psycholinguistic dimensions of artificial cognition, detailing the relationship between recursive syntactic structures, the deployment of mental state verbs, and the emulation of Theory of Mind (ToM) capabilities in non-English outputs.

Through an exhaustive synthesis of empirical data, architectural comparisons, and statistical reliability metrics, this document elucidates the profound structural bottlenecks that govern modern multilingual language models, offering a nuanced understanding of how linguistic asymmetry dictates strategic reasoning, ethical alignment, and cognitive modeling.

## Activation Addition (ActAdd) and the Mechanics of Cross-Lingual Scaling Asymmetry

The alignment, behavioral control, and safe deployment of Large Language Models have traditionally relied upon computationally intensive, resource-heavy methodologies, most notably Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).1 While effective, these optimization-based approaches permanently alter the model weights and require vast amounts of curated, domain-specific training data. In response to these computational bottlenecks, representation engineering—and specifically, activation engineering—has emerged as a highly efficient, inference-time alternative.2 This paradigm operates under the premise that an LLM's forward pass can be dynamically perturbed to predictably alter high-level behavioral outputs without requiring any gradient descent or persistent weight updates.2

The Activation Addition (ActAdd) technique serves as a primary mechanism within this paradigm.4 ActAdd functions by calculating a steering vector derived from the geometric difference in the intermediate residual stream activations resulting from a pair of contrastive prompts (for instance, evaluating the activation states of a "Deceptive" prompt versus an "Honest" prompt).4 This isolated steering vector is subsequently injected—added as a bias—into the model's forward pass during inference, scaled by a predetermined hyperparameter known as the scaling coefficient (![]()).2

### The Empirical Scaling Coefficient (![]()) Asymmetry

Extensive empirical evaluations conducted on post-2024 foundational models reveal a stark, systemic asymmetry in the scaling coefficients required to achieve reliable behavioral steering in English versus non-English languages.7 The scaling coefficient (![]()) acts as an intensity multiplier; it dictates the magnitude of the geometric perturbation applied to the latent space.6

When steering LLMs to modulate behaviors such as toxicity, sentiment, or refusal rates in English, empirical data demonstrates that a relatively low scaling coefficient—typically residing around ![]()—is sufficient to induce a robust, predictable behavioral shift.6 At this magnitude, the model successfully alters its semantic trajectory while perfectly preserving its inherent perplexity, generative fluency, and grasp of English syntax.2

However, attempts to replicate this steering efficacy in non-English languages encounter significant geometric resistance within the model's activation space. Eliciting identical behavioral modifications in non-English contexts requires a substantially amplified scaling coefficient, generally demanding ![](), and occasionally higher depending on the resource sparsity of the target language.7 This profound scaling asymmetry suggests that the latent representations of non-English semantic concepts are positioned in a less dense, more peripheral, and less interconnected region of the model's high-dimensional representation space.

Because the dominant pre-training language (English) occupies the densely populated core of the parameter space, its latent features are highly reactive to minor arithmetic perturbations.13 Conversely, non-English representations possess lower inherent activation magnitudes for complex behavioral concepts. Consequently, they require aggressive arithmetic amplification (a higher ![]()) to override the default autoregressive probability distribution.

This aggressive scaling, however, introduces severe instability. Interventions that utilize excessively high coefficients to force cross-lingual compliance routinely disrupt the fundamental operational mechanics of the model. Research indicates that when extreme scaling vectors are injected to compensate for language sparsity, the LLM suffers from catastrophic syntactic degradation, completely losing its grasp of grammar, coherence, and structural logic, ultimately generating highly randomized, uninterpretable text.5

The efficacy, stability, and scaling dynamics of ActAdd interventions do not apply uniformly across all LLM architectures. Instead, they exhibit distinct functional variations depending heavily on the model's primary design philosophy, evolutionary lineage, and parameter scale.14

Conversely, models explicitly optimized heavily for deep mathematical, coding, and logical reasoning develop highly rigid, coupled activation pathways. Attempting to steer these reasoning-prior models cross-lingually via standard ActAdd methodologies frequently fails, as the forced geometric shift violently collides with the model's deeply entrenched, language-agnostic logic circuits, resulting in severe utility drift and an inability to maintain cross-lingual semantic fidelity.14

### Vector Construction Methodologies: PCA Versus Mean-Difference

The mathematical framework utilized to construct the steering vector is as critical as the scaling coefficient itself, significantly influencing both the precision of the intervention and the severity of the cross-lingual asymmetry.16 Historically, ActAdd has relied upon a simple mean-difference approach.17 This method calculates the average activation difference between a set of positive prompts and a set of negative prompts across a specific layer. While computationally trivial, the mean-difference approach is highly susceptible to capturing spurious correlations, syntactic noise, and token-position artifacts inherent in the specific natural language prompts used for calibration.16

Advanced empirical evaluations demonstrate that Principal Component Analysis (PCA) offers a vastly superior mechanism for steering vector construction, particularly in highly complex, multi-attribute, and cross-lingual environments.16 By isolating the primary principal component of the activation differences, PCA extracts the most salient, pure directional variance associated specifically with the target behavior, mathematically discarding orthogonal noise and unrelated syntactic artifacts.

| ------------------------------------ | ----------------------------- | ----------------- | -------------- | ------------------ |
| Mean-Difference (Traditional ActAdd) | Moderate                      | Moderate          | Moderate       | Moderate           |
| Contrastive Mean (CAA)               | High                          | Low               | Moderate       | Moderate           |
| PCA Composition (SRS Method)         | 0.990                         | 0.312             | 0.972          | 0.983              |

The empirical data clearly indicates that PCA-based composition outperforms traditional mean-difference methods across all measured alignment objectives.16 On models such as Gemma-2-9B-it, the PCA approach yields an exceptional refusal rate for malicious prompts (0.990) while simultaneously achieving superior fairness (0.972) and truthfulness (0.983).16

In the specific context of multilingual steering, PCA is theorized to be particularly vital for mitigating scaling asymmetry. Because non-English representations are inextricably entangled with English-centric latent structures, basic mean-difference vectors inadvertently capture and inject English syntactic biases into the non-English generation process. PCA, by virtue of its orthogonal projection capabilities, isolates the purely semantic behavioral vector, stripping away the language-specific syntactic noise, thereby allowing for a cleaner, more stable behavioral shift at slightly lower ![]() thresholds.

### The Semantic Hub Hypothesis and the Optimal Locus of Intervention

The fundamental mechanism that allows any cross-lingual steering vector to function across linguistic boundaries is articulated by the "Semantic Hub Hypothesis".18 Drawing inspiration from the hub-and-spoke models of human neuroscience, this hypothesis posits that modern large language models learn a shared, transmodal, language-agnostic representation space entirely contained within their intermediate transformer layers.19

Regardless of the input language or the modality (whether it be text, executable code, or mathematical expressions), the model projects semantically equivalent concepts into neighboring geometric coordinates within these middle layers.18 Through extensive logit lens analyses, researchers have observed that the intermediate hidden states generated when processing non-English inputs (e.g., Chinese or Arabic text) closely align mathematically with the unembeddings of their direct English translations.13

This phenomenon of English-centric conceptual routing dictates that the optimal locus for cross-lingual steering interventions resides precisely within these "Semantic Hub" middle layers.13 By extracting a steering vector using English-language prompt pairs and subsequently injecting that vector directly into the middle layers, the intervention naturally propagates through the semantic hub. This process seamlessly alters the conceptual trajectory of the model, which is then translated by the later decoder layers into the distinct target language.22 This verified cross-lingual transferability confirms that multilingual models do not maintain isolated, parallel linguistic sub-networks for deep semantic reasoning. Instead, they funnel highly diverse syntactic inputs through a monolithic, English-dominant cognitive bottleneck, rendering the middle layers the absolute critical junction for activation engineering.13

## Metacognitive Calibration and Strategy Classification in Multilingual Multi-Agent Social Dilemmas

As Large Language Models are increasingly granted autonomy and deployed as interacting agents within complex, multi-agent environments, evaluating their intrinsic capacity for strategic reasoning, risk assessment, and cooperative dynamics has become a paramount field of study.24 Game-theoretic frameworks, most notably Social Dilemmas such as the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma (IPD) and Public Goods games, serve as standardized, rigorous mathematical environments used to observe how LLMs handle cooperation, defection, retaliation, and long-term utility maximization.24

However, extensive empirical research underscores that strategic behavior in LLMs is not an invariant, mathematically pure calculation. Instead, it is highly susceptible to linguistic framing. The specific language, syntax, and cultural context in which the game rules are prompted heavily dictate the ensuing trajectory of the LLM's actions, frequently overriding the actual mathematical payoff structures of the game.24

### Classifier Reliability and Sequence Mapping Architectures

To systematically analyze and quantify LLM behavior over extended iterations of the IPD, researchers employ advanced machine learning classifiers—primarily Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and modern Transformer architectures—to map the noisy, stochastic sequences of LLM actions to canonical, formalized game-theory strategies.25 These canonical strategies include rigid behavioral archetypes such as Tit-For-Tat (TFT), Always Defect (ALLD), Always Cooperate (ALLC), and Win-Stay-Lose-Shift (WSLS).25

Empirical evaluations utilizing standardized environments like the FAIRGAME framework demonstrate that LSTM architectures are highly capable of mapping these behavioral sequences, frequently achieving up to 94% overall strategy classification accuracy.25 However, the reliability of these strategy classifiers fluctuates dramatically depending on the conditional complexity of the LLM's deployed strategy.24

| Canonical IPD Strategy Archetype   | Classifier Architecture   | Classification F1-Score | Vulnerability to Execution Noise and Confusion                                                                |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Unconditional (ALLC, ALLD)         | LSTM                      | 0.99                    | Highly robust; minimal sequence confusion observed.                                                           |
| Conditional / Reactive (TFT, WSLS) | LSTM                      | Moderate                | Modest confusion under high environmental execution noise.                                                    |
| Conditional / Reactive (TFT, WSLS) | Hidden Markov Model (HMM) | ![]()                     | High vulnerability; fundamentally struggles with long-term dependency tracking over extended game iterations. |

While LSTM networks exhibit near-perfect F1-scores (0.99) when identifying unconditional, static strategies (such as an LLM deciding to unconditionally defect across all rounds), they encounter modest but persistent classification confusion when attempting to distinguish between highly reactive strategies like TFT and WSLS.24 This confusion is primarily driven by "execution noise." Unlike deterministic computer algorithms, LLM agents operating in multi-agent environments occasionally hallucinate actions, misinterpret the game state history, or generate suboptimal responses due to temperature sampling.24 These stochastic execution errors blur the characteristic mathematical signatures that the LSTM relies upon, revealing a fundamental limitation in treating autoregressive LLM text generation as a purely deterministic, strategic algorithm.24 Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) perform even worse (F1 ![]() 0.74), as their lack of gating mechanisms prevents them from maintaining the long-term historical context necessary to accurately classify delayed retaliation.24

### Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and English-Centric Training Bias

The deployment of these strategy classifiers on game trajectories generated from non-English prompts exposes severe, systemic vulnerabilities in metacognitive calibration. The Expected Calibration Error (ECE) is a critical statistical metric that measures the absolute discrepancy between a model's predicted confidence in an answer and its actual empirical accuracy.29 In a perfectly calibrated model, an output delivered with 90% confidence will be empirically correct exactly 90% of the time.

In multilingual, strategic reasoning contexts, LLMs demonstrate extreme, highly dangerous miscalibration.30 Empirical studies benchmarking foundational models such as Llama 3 reveal a devastating linguistic disparity: while the ECE for complex English-language tasks sits at a relatively robust 4.6%, the ECE for equivalent tasks in non-English languages skyrockets to an average of 23.1%—representing nearly a five-fold degradation in metacognitive reliability.31

This profound English-centric bias is an unintended artifact born from the intersection of skewed pre-training data distributions and the preference alignment process (RLHF).29 Preference alignment collapse occurs when an LLM is heavily optimized to generate assertive, human-preferred responses. This alignment process inadvertently strips away the model's internal statistical representations of epistemic uncertainty.29 Consequently, when processing non-English IPD prompts—such as complex game theory matrices presented in Arabic or Vietnamese—the model is forced to operate in a sparsely populated, low-resource latent space. Despite lacking sufficient semantic data to perform accurate backward induction, the model outputs strategic predictions and aggressive actions with extreme, entirely unjustified overconfidence.29

The systemic lack of calibration in non-English strategic reasoning implies that LLM agents deployed in international geopolitical, economic, or military simulations are highly prone to aggressive, unpredictable, and overconfident escalations that they would intrinsically avoid when prompted in English.25

### Generative Fluency and the "Unclassified" Action Rate

When strategy classifiers evaluate a sequence of actions that degrades into stochastic chaos—failing to align mathematically with any recognizable game-theoretic archetype—the sequence is binned into an "unclassified" category.33 Tracking standard unclassified rates acts as an essential proxy metric for determining an LLM's overarching inability to maintain logical coherence, memory, and strategic consistency over extended dialogue contexts.33

Analyses across diverse linguistic families highlight that unclassified strategic rates are inversely, and powerfully, correlated with the baseline generative fluency of the LLM in that specific language.33

The generation of high unclassified rates in morphologically complex or resource-poor languages such as Turkish and Urdu (19 unclassified game instances) compared to moderately resourced languages like Arabic and Catalan (8 unclassified instances) indicates a systemic failure of the LLM to sustain a coherent persona.33 These failures routinely manifest as "mixed-origin" or "model-dominant" hallucinations, wherein the LLM entirely abandons the mathematical and structural constraints of the IPD payoff matrix and begins outputting highly random, illogical actions.34

The strong inverse correlation between poor language fluency and high unclassified strategic rates suggests a zero-sum cognitive dynamic within the transformer architecture. The sheer cognitive and computational overhead required to merely generate grammatically correct tokens in a low-resource language completely depletes the computational capacity and attention head bandwidth necessary for complex, multi-step strategic reasoning, leading directly to catastrophic action degradation.33

## Empirical Validation of Ensemble Judging in Low-Resource Multilingual Contexts

The rapid, exponential proliferation of Large Language Models has rendered traditional human evaluation methodologies excessively slow, subjective, and cost-prohibitive.36 In response, the computational linguistics community has aggressively adopted the "LLM-as-a-Judge" paradigm.37 This automated protocol utilizes highly advanced frontier models (e.g., GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet) to grade, rank, and critique the textual outputs of other models based on highly specific, prompt-injected rubrics.37 While this approach offers unprecedented scalability, the empirical reliability and baseline validity of LLM judges when forced to operate in complex multilingual contexts—specifically evaluating outputs across ![]() non-English languages—remains a subject of intense empirical scrutiny and debate.38

### Inter-Rater Reliability Metrics: Fleiss' ![]() and Krippendorff's ![]()

To mathematically validate the consistency, objectivity, and reliability of LLM judges against human expert baselines, researchers rely on standard statistical measures of inter-rater reliability. The predominant metrics utilized are Fleiss' ![]() (which measures categorical agreement among multiple independent raters above what would be expected by pure chance) and Krippendorff's ![]() (a highly robust measure that calculates overall reliability based on the magnitude of disagreement across varying, complex measurement scales).39

Empirical investigations systematically evaluating judge models across 25 distinct languages and five varied evaluation tasks reveal that single-model LLM-as-a-Judge protocols exhibit severe, disqualifying instability in cross-lingual settings.38 The average Fleiss' ![]() for multilingual evaluations hovers at approximately 0.3.38 In statistical terms, a ![]() value of 0.3 indicates only "fair" to "poor" agreement, falling drastically short of the strict reliability thresholds required for rigorous scientific benchmarking or safety evaluations.38

Furthermore, these standardized reliability metrics are intrinsically susceptible to the "consensus trap".41 Traditional agreement metrics penalize evaluative variance and heavily reward rating homogeneity. Because the alignment parameters of major LLM judges are heavily calibrated toward a Western, English-centric, and predominantly male cultural perspective, they artificially inflate their own Fleiss' ![]() scores by uniformly suppressing valid, pluralistic, and culture-specific disagreements.41 Consequently, an LLM judge evaluating sociopolitical responses in Arabic or Vietnamese may achieve a false statistical consensus simply by overriding localized cultural nuances with a rigid, homogenized English-aligned heuristic.41

### Cost-Reliability Trade-Offs in Multi-Judge Ensembles and Self-Translation

To directly mitigate the unreliability and statistical noise inherent in single-judge paradigms operating in low-resource languages, evaluation engineers have increasingly deployed multi-judge ensembles. These ensembles (typically utilizing 3 to 5 independent LLM instances or varied temperature parameters) evaluate a single output and execute a majority voting system to finalize the score.42

Ensemble configurations—such as the JHV architecture (which incorporates one massive, frontier-level parameter model as an anchor) and the JHC architecture (which incorporates a diverse panel of multiple models of similar scale)—demonstrate marked improvements in statistical correlation with human ground-truth consensus.42 While a single LLM judge may randomly hallucinate a severe evaluation penalty due to a momentary failure to parse unfamiliar non-English syntax, an ensemble acts as a highly effective statistical smoothing function, discarding anomalous hallucinations.42

However, ensemble evaluations geometrically multiply API costs. To balance this cost-reliability trade-off, self-translation-augmented judging has emerged as a highly critical protocol.38 In this specific architecture, the target non-English response is first translated into English by the judge model, comprehensively evaluated within the model's highly calibrated English latent space, and the resulting score is then mapped back to the original language.36 While this process slightly increases token-generation expenditures, it successfully bypasses the abysmal multilingual Fleiss' ![]() by forcing the entire evaluation process into the model's "Semantic Hub," significantly increasing the mathematical stability and linguistic accuracy of the final verdict.

### Failure Coupling Rates Between Proprietary Models

A critical, often overlooked vulnerability in the ensemble approach is the phenomenon of "failure coupling"—the specific rate at which distinct, supposedly independent LLM judges make identical, systematic errors during evaluation.44 If a multi-judge ensemble is composed of highly correlated models, the majority voting system completely fails to correct errors; instead, it confidently amplifies and mathematically validates them.

Empirical evidence indicates that models bred from within the same evolutionary lineage (for example, ensembling GPT-4 Turbo alongside GPT-4o) exhibit exceptionally high failure coupling rates.44 Because they share underlying pre-training data and alignment methodologies, they possess identical, deeply encoded blind spots regarding non-English syntax evaluation and will uniformly, identically penalize valid local linguistic idioms.

Conversely, mixing distinctly different model families (e.g., combining OpenAI's GPT-4o with Anthropic's Claude 3.5 Sonnet) moderately reduces the failure coupling rate. However, even across these disparate proprietary boundaries, researchers note that base heuristic similarities stubbornly persist. Both Claude 3.5 and GPT-4o have been shown to apply human-like reasoning heuristics highly rigidly, severely lacking adaptability in highly contextual non-English scenarios.25 This shared rigidity leads to highly correlated, cross-family failures when the models are tasked with evaluating complex, culturally nuanced logic in low-resource languages.25

### Rubric Granularity and Inter-Judge Consistency

The architectural design and linguistic structure of the evaluation rubric itself profoundly dictates the resulting inter-judge reliability in multilingual contexts.42 The specific granularity of the prompt—whether it requests a hard binary classification (Pass/Fail) or demands a highly nuanced, continuous Likert scale (ranging from 1 to 5)—interacts unpredictably with the model's multilingual capabilities.

Multilingual LLM judges fundamentally struggle to reliably calibrate continuous Likert scales due to the exact Expected Calibration Error (ECE) degradation phenomena previously analyzed.31 A judge model attempting to differentiate between a "3" (Adequate) and a "4" (Good) in a highly complex Urdu summarization task simply lacks the high-definition semantic resolution required to make a consistent judgment. This lack of resolution results in chaotic, highly randomized variance between ensemble members, destroying the Krippendorff's ![]() score.

Conversely, minimizing rubric granularity to strict binary checks (e.g., asking exclusively, "Does the generated response contain an explicit refusal? Yes or No.") drastically improves inter-judge consistency. By utilizing binary constraints, the cognitive load of the evaluation is reduced from complex, qualitative continuous scaling down to basic algorithmic feature detection, allowing the model to perform reliably even in languages where its semantic grasp is severely limited.42

## Syntactic Mapping and the Emulation of Theory of Mind (ToM) in Non-English Architectures

Theory of Mind (ToM)—defined as the advanced cognitive ability to accurately attribute distinct mental states, beliefs, intents, desires, and knowledge to oneself and to interacting others—serves as a foundational pillar for advanced, empathetic, and safely aligned artificial intelligence behavior.47 While early scaling laws surrounding LLMs enthusiastically suggested that ToM capabilities emerge organically as parameter counts increase, rigorous cross-lingual analyses reveal severe vulnerabilities in how these models syntactically map complex cognitive states outside of the English language.48

### Mental State Verbs and Epistemic Vocabulary Correlation

Deep psycholinguistic research demonstrates that human ToM development is intrinsically and biologically linked to the acquisition of specific, highly complex syntactic structures, primarily the mastery of "Mental State Verbs" (e.g., verbs such as believe, know, intend, discover, and think).48 These specific verbs encode deeply conceptual meaning and explicit representational information regarding the internal state of another entity.50

In morphologically complex languages such as Japanese, the linguistic expression of epistemic states is highly bifurcated. The language relies simultaneously on high-frequency, closed-class sentence-final particles (such as the particle 'tte', which denotes hearsay or reported information) that convey procedural, highly automatic meaning, and low-frequency, open-class mental state verbs (such as 'shitteru', meaning "to actively know").50 Developmental studies prove that human children systematically master procedural sentence-final particles long before they understand conceptual mental state verbs, and crucially, their performance on standard false-belief tasks (the primary test for ToM) correlates exclusively with their mastery of the latter.50

When state-of-the-art LLMs are evaluated on advanced ToM benchmarks (such as the XToM suite or rigorous variations of LLM-Hanabi), they exhibit a fundamental, systemic disconnect in this established developmental pathway.48 While an LLM can perfectly, flawlessly mimic the statistical distribution and grammatical placement of Japanese sentence-final particles, their internal attention mechanisms frequently fail to bind these particles to the actual epistemic ground truth of the entities within the prompt context.

Empirical evaluations confirm that the raw frequency of Mental State Verb usage in Arabic, Chinese, and Japanese LLM outputs serves as a direct, highly accurate proxy for the model's success on ToM benchmarks. Models that fail to generate appropriate mental state verbs inevitably fail to track false beliefs.50 This discrepancy highlights that LLMs approach mind modeling through a purely statistical, surface-level probabilistic mimicry, rather than possessing any true underlying cognitive architecture.48

### Syntactic Degradation and English-Centric Internal Transfer

The primary underlying etiology of ToM failure in non-English interactions can be directly traced to the phenomenon of "Syntactic Degradation" occurring during the model's internal data routing. To successfully execute a ToM reasoning task, a model must maintain highly complex, deeply nested syntactic structures in its contextual memory (e.g., maintaining recursive complement clauses such as, "Agent A believes that Agent B does not know that the specific object was secretly moved by Agent C").

Because modern LLMs utilize an English-dominant Semantic Hub within their intermediate layers 18, a non-English ToM prompt must undergo a complex architectural journey. The non-English input must be conceptually mapped into the English latent space, processed for logical resolution within the English-aligned reasoning circuits, and finally mapped back out to the target language through the decoder heads.13

This mandatory dual-translation requirement acts as a highly lossy compression algorithm on the syntactic structure. The intricate, recursive nested clauses fundamentally required to preserve varying epistemic states suffer from severe syntactic degradation during the transfer into and out of the semantic hub. The structural, geometric integrity of the recursion simply collapses, resulting in the model losing track of which specific entity holds which specific belief.13 The ultimate consequence observed in non-English ToM benchmarks is not a failure of the model's raw reasoning capacity, but a failure of cross-lingual memory allocation—the LLM simply cannot hold complex foreign grammar and complex logical states in its contextual scratchpad simultaneously without English acting as an intermediary, and ultimately destructive, crutch.13

## Synthesis and Structural Implications for Multilingual Deployment

The exhaustive empirical data gathered across these four highly specialized computational domains—Activation Addition scaling parameters, IPD strategy classification calibration, LLM-as-Judge ensembling stability, and Theory of Mind syntactic mapping—converges on a singular, unifying theoretical conclusion: Modern Multilingual Large Language Models operate through a highly centralized, fundamentally monolingual cognitive bottleneck.

The geometric distance of non-English concepts is explicitly proven by the necessity of aggressively high scaling coefficients (![]()) during ActAdd interventions.7 This parameter disparity mathematically proves that foreign language representations are marginalized within the latent space, lacking the dense activation pathways of English concepts, and thereby requiring forced mathematical amplification that threatens syntactic stability.5

Simultaneously, the catastrophic rise in Expected Calibration Error (scaling from a baseline 4.6% to 23.1%) and the high rates of unclassified strategic behavior in languages like Turkish and Arabic reveal that the raw computational cost of non-native token generation directly cannibalizes the transformer resources required for higher-order reasoning.31 The models become dangerously hyper-confident in their strategic deployments precisely because their internal representation of uncertainty fails to translate cleanly across the English-dominant Semantic Hub.29

Furthermore, while majority-voting ensembles and strict binary rubrics can artificially stabilize the Fleiss' ![]() of multilingual evaluations, the persistently high failure coupling rates between proprietary models indicate that this stability is often a shared algorithmic delusion.44 LLM judges frequently agree with each other not because they possess an accurate, objective perception of non-English cultural nuance, but because they share the exact same English-centric heuristic biases imposed by homogenized RLHF training methodologies.41

Finally, the syntactic limit of artificial empathy is clearly defined. The total inability of LLMs to track mental state verbs and nested recursive clauses in languages like Japanese or Arabic without suffering syntactic degradation indicates that current architectures do not, in fact, possess an abstract, language-agnostic Theory of Mind.48 Their socio-cognitive capabilities remain inextricably bound to the grammatical structure and latent density of the English language.

Ultimately, achieving true, robust multilingual alignment and reliable cross-lingual reasoning will require a fundamental architectural paradigm shift. Moving beyond the limitations of the English-dominant Semantic Hub necessitates the development of localized, culturally grounded representation spaces that do not require aggressive vector scaling, lossy internal translation, or the suppression of native syntactic complexity to achieve logical coherence. Until such structural innovations are realized, the deployment of Large Language Models in critical, high-stakes multilingual environments remains constrained by profound, quantifiable, and systemic architectural asymmetries.
