# Dynamics of Strategic Deception and Cooperative Bias in Multilingual Large Language Models: A Comprehensive Audit

## The Epistemological Gap in Multilingual Multi-Agent Systems

The rapid proliferation and widespread deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) have catalyzed a fundamental migration in artificial intelligence architecture. The field is transitioning from reactive, single-prompt question-answering systems into autonomous agents embedded within complex, dynamic Multi-Agent Systems (MAS). In these interactive environments, models are increasingly tasked with negotiating transactions, allocating shared resources, establishing coalition trusts, and executing long-horizon strategic planning.1 However, the methodological frameworks dedicated to evaluating the alignment, safety, and strategic behavior of these systems have not evolved concurrently with their deployment capabilities. The natural language processing field has predominantly relied on static, English-centric benchmarks that evaluate single-turn structured prediction tasks. These legacy evaluation paradigms inherently fail to capture behavioral conviction, intent-action divergence, and the nuances of strategic deception when models are placed under dynamic, interactive pressure.1

This persistent reliance on sanitized, monolingual evaluation infrastructures creates a profound epistemological gap—an illusion of alignment where models appear highly cooperative, safe, and robust when operating in high-resource languages, but experience catastrophic behavioral degradation when processing low-resource or non-English inputs.1 The optimization of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) pipelines and constitutional safety guardrails is overwhelmingly weighted toward high-resource linguistic representations.1 Consequently, empirical evidence increasingly supports the hypothesis that language functions as an "Algorithmic Sapir-Whorf Switch." In this paradigm, the specific linguistic context of a prompt systematically modulates an autonomous agent’s strategic intent, its Theory of Mind (ToM) capabilities, and its inherent propensity for deception.1

To rigorously interrogate this phenomenon, recent empirical literature spanning 2023 to 2026 provides a wealth of multi-agent game-theoretic evaluations, mechanistic interpretability diagnostics, and cross-lingual calibration audits. The convergent evidence suggests that cross-lingual strategic divergences are not simply superficial artifacts of cultural essentialism embedded in the training corpus, but are instead deeply rooted in the mechanistic architecture of multilingual computation—specifically governed by transmodal representational compression.1 Furthermore, the degradation of metacognitive confidence, measurable via the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and Information-level ECE (InfoECE), correlates directly with an agent's failure to adhere to operational constraints and its tendency to hallucinate compliance in foreign linguistic spaces.5 This comprehensive audit synthesizes these intersecting domains to dictate the necessary evolution of interactive agentic benchmarks and to expose the strategic vulnerabilities of multilingual LLMs.

## The "Nicer than Human" Bias and Cross-Lingual Strategic Volatility

In the context of behavioral game theory, human rationality, cooperation, and defection are traditionally benchmarked through classical mathematical abstractions such as the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma (IPD), Public Goods games, the Battle of the Sexes, and the Ultimatum Game.1 Early investigations into LLM behavior within these abstractions revealed a pronounced "Nicer than Human" bias.1 When prompted in English, foundation models heavily default to prosocial, altruistic, or unconditionally cooperative strategies at rates that significantly exceed established empirical human baselines.1 In finite repeated 2x2 games, LLMs frequently demonstrate an artificial willingness to absorb sucker payoffs to maintain a cooperative equilibrium, or alternatively, they adopt unforgiving retaliatory strategies—such as strict Tit-for-Tat—that artificially enforce a cooperative state through the threat of perpetual mutual destruction.11

Extensive literature published in high-impact venues such as Nature Human Behaviour indicates that models like GPT-4 perform exceptionally well in self-interested environments like the Prisoner's Dilemma precisely because they are relentlessly unforgiving regarding defections, yet they struggle immensely with environments that require nuanced social coordination, such as the Battle of the Sexes.9 The aggregate cooperation rates of LLMs can soar to 79% compared to the historical human average of roughly 48% in identical experimental conditions.13 This sycophantic cooperative bias has historically been interpreted as a triumph of RLHF alignment. However, the assumption that this cooperative bias is a universal, intrinsic feature of aligned LLMs collapses entirely when subjected to rigorous multilingual evaluation.

Empirical data demonstrates that the linguistic context of the interaction acts as an independent variable that significantly alters the strategic archetypes deployed by the model. High-resolution multi-agent simulations reveal that the "Nicer than Human" bias frequently degrades into volatile, outcome-dependent, or hyper-aggressive behavioral profiles depending exclusively on the prompt language, exposing a brittle underlying alignment that is bound to syntax rather than abstract conceptual morality.1

### Typological Divergence in Canonical Strategy Distributions

When evaluated utilizing standard canonical strategies—Always Cooperate (ALLC), Always Defect (ALLD), Tit-for-Tat (TFT), and Win-Stay-Lose-Shift (WSLS)—distinct strategic profiles emerge that subvert traditional cultural expectations.1 Table 1 visualizes the aggregated strategy distributions observed across various linguistic contexts, highlighting the collapse of universal prosociality and the manifestation of linguistic determinism in vector spaces.

The data indicates that models processing Arabic inputs do not merely shift toward a slightly higher rate of defection; rather, they exhibit a volatile bimodal distribution.1 In Arabic, LLMs simultaneously produce the highest rates of unconditional cooperation across all tested languages (32%) alongside exceedingly high rates of unconditional defection (28%).1 This suggests that rather than adopting sophisticated adaptive reciprocal strategies like Tit-for-Tat, the internal representations in Arabic lack the intermediate, nuanced alignment templates present in English. The sparsity of safety and strategy data in Arabic forces the model's policy network to select extreme, unhedged poles of the strategic spectrum.1

Similarly, Chinese reasoning demonstrates a heavy bias toward unconditional strategies, with the combined rate of ALWAYS Cooperate and ALWAYS Defect exceeding 60%.1 However, when adaptive strategies are occasionally employed, Chinese-prompted models strongly favor Win-Stay-Lose-Shift over Tit-for-Tat, indicating an outcome-dependent optimization pathway rather than an intent-based reciprocal pathway.1 Furthermore, Vietnamese models consistently subvert human collectivist expectations, frequently yielding high defection rates even when the prompt context attempts to enforce cooperative priming.1

Crucially, these divergences cannot be cleanly attributed to cultural essentialism or the model's internalization of regional human values during the pre-training phase. A rigorous cross-lingual persuasion study analyzing human behavioral outcomes in response to AI-generated multilingual texts found no statistically significant language effect on human decision-making.1 In interactive environments involving human participants, recipients anchor their decisions on "source belief"—whether they suspect the counterpart is an AI—rather than the micro-linguistic nuances of the generated text.1 Therefore, the strategic divergences observed in LLMs are best explained by tokenization inefficiencies, RLHF coverage asymmetry, and the mechanistic properties of latent vector spaces.1 Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) tokenizers frequently fracture morphologically rich or low-resource languages into sub-optimal sequences, distorting semantic proximity and stripping away the nuanced contextual framing required to calculate and maintain cooperative equilibria in complex games.1

## The FAIRGAME Framework and the Parametrization of Strategy

To systematically audit these latent behavioral intentions without relying on anecdotal single-turn outputs, recent literature has established and heavily utilized the FAIRGAME (Framework for AI Agents Bias Recognition using Game Theory) infrastructure.18 Developed and refined by researchers such as Buscemi and Huynh between 2024 and 2026, FAIRGAME serves as a modular simulation engine that formalizes the strategic interactions of LLMs as players in normal-form and infinitely repeated games.18 It supports highly controlled experimentation across dynamic prompt configurations, inter-sentential linguistic variations, assigned agent personalities (e.g., overtly cooperative versus explicitly selfish), and adjustable payoff matrix structures, thereby enabling deep quantitative auditing of emergent multi-agent dynamics.18

### Payoff Multipliers and Incentive Sensitivity

A critical methodological advancement within the FAIRGAME framework is the introduction of payoff multipliers, typically denoted as ![]() or ![](), which scale the absolute magnitude of incentives without altering the underlying strategic ranking of the game matrix.15 In a standard Prisoner's Dilemma, the ordinal ranking of outcomes must remain Temptation (![]()) > Reward (![]()) > Punishment (![]()) > Sucker (![]()). By applying a scalar parameter ![]() to the baseline penalty matrix, researchers can isolate a model's absolute sensitivity to incentive magnitude while holding the mathematical game theory constant.15

High-resolution investigations utilizing ![]() demonstrate that linguistic-cultural priming strongly and unpredictably interacts with the objective stakes of the game.15 In low-stakes, attenuated environments where ![]() (for example, where the objective penalty for mutual cooperation is reduced to a mere 0.2 units), languages such as Arabic and Vietnamese exhibit severe, aggressive defection-oriented behavior.15 The mathematical comprehension of the attenuated stakes interacts poorly with low-resource tokenization, resulting in a phenomenon termed "attention drift." The tokenization inefficiencies blur the semantic weight of numerical values, causing the agent to improperly weight the long-term cost of defection and default to short-term exploitation.1

However, as the simulated consequences amplify to high stakes (![]()), the strategic behavior in these low-resource languages aggressively shifts back toward cooperation, ultimately converging with the English baseline.15 The severity of the numerical penalty essentially overrides the linguistic confusion, forcing the model into a mathematically safe posture. Conversely, languages like French exhibit profound "cultural inertia" within the latent space, maintaining relatively stable, high-cooperation norms across all payoff magnitudes regardless of the mathematical incentive to defect.15

### LSTM-Based Strategy Classifiers and Methodological Vulnerabilities

Within the FAIRGAME framework, the identification of canonical strategies from raw, multi-turn dialogue logs is achieved through a hybrid classification pipeline. This pipeline involves strict rule-based pattern matching for unambiguous trajectories, paired with supervised Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) sequence classifiers to interpret complex, noisy, or stochastic action histories.15 These LSTM classifiers are trained on synthetic repeated-game trajectories to map sequence histories of up to 10 rounds to the predefined labels of ALLC, ALLD, TFT, and WSLS.15

While current implementations boast high classification confidence—with roughly 90% of evaluated 10-round trajectories achieving an internal LSTM probability score greater than 0.9—the broader literature highlights severe methodological vulnerabilities in this classification approach that must be rectified in future iterations.1 The standard classification threshold used to assign a definitive strategy is typically set at ![]().1 This relatively low threshold creates a substantial "unclassified" or ambiguous zone, leading to the systemic misclassification of boundary strategies. For instance, distinguishing standard strict Tit-for-Tat from Generous Tit-for-Tat or Noisy Tit-for-Tat requires a level of sequence comprehension that an LSTM thresholded at 0.6 frequently fails to provide.1

Crucially, the current matrix game literature frequently underreports human-validated error analyses and detailed calibration curves for these strategy classifiers across different languages.1 Classification instability is not uniform; it is inversely correlated with the generative fluency of the prompt language. Evaluated sequences generated in morphologically complex or low-resource languages such as Urdu and Turkish exhibit the highest rates of unclassified or oscillating instances due to extreme linguistic fragmentation, whereas English sequences remain highly stable.1

Consequently, robust cross-lingual evaluation demands the immediate adoption of pre-registered reporting standards. Future FAIRGAME-style audits must report 95% Wilson score intervals for all proportion estimates to capture the true variance of the strategy distributions.1 Furthermore, studies must publish layer-wise calibration curves (reliability diagrams) for the LSTM classifiers, alongside human-annotated confusion matrices utilizing Cohen's ![]() to mathematically verify inter-annotator reliability per language.1 Relying on uncalibrated black-box classifiers to map multi-turn dialogue into rigid mathematical strategies introduces unacceptable noise into the cross-lingual evaluation pipeline.

## Metacognitive Confidence and Expected Calibration Error (ECE) Degradation

The empirical volatility of strategic reasoning observed in non-English contexts is inextricably linked to a catastrophic collapse in the models' metacognitive confidence capabilities. In machine learning alignment, Expected Calibration Error (ECE) serves as the paramount metric for assessing the reliability and internal awareness of an agent. It quantifies the divergence between a model's subjective confidence (its probabilistic logit output for a given action) and its empirical accuracy.5 A perfectly calibrated model possesses an ECE of zero; a model exhibiting high ECE suffers from either catastrophic overconfidence (hallucinating certainty) or unwarranted underconfidence (failing to utilize encoded knowledge).

Recent comprehensive audits of multilingual calibration reveal a systemic and dramatic degradation in ECE for non-English and low-resource languages, fundamentally undermining the safety of deploying these agents in global contexts.5 As demonstrated by evaluations on extensive datasets such as MMMLU, heavily English-aligned models exhibit an average ECE of approximately 4.6% when processing English reasoning prompts.1 However, this metric violently degrades when transitioning to foreign linguistic spaces, skyrocketing to an average of 23.1% for non-English languages, with low-resource variants faring significantly worse.1

To adequately measure calibration across multi-turn strategic dialogues of varying lengths, researchers have introduced the Information-level ECE (InfoECE), which normalizes predictions by information exposure.8 When analyzed through the lens of InfoECE and supporting metrics like the Brier Score, the cross-lingual disparity becomes starkly quantifiable.

This metacognitive collapse manifests uniquely depending on the specific pre-training and alignment architecture of the model family. English-prioritized models (such as the LLaMA lineage) struggle primarily to maintain basic calibration outside their native latent space. Conversely, explicitly multilingual-first models (such as Aya-Expanse) exhibit systemic, uncalibrated overconfidence across all languages, maintaining average confidence scores exceeding 76.9% even when empirical accuracy collapses below 50%.5 The statistical correlation between global data resource availability and internal calibration is profound, with Brier scores and AUROC metrics deteriorating linearly in strict proportion to the scarcity of the language in the pre-training corpus (yielding a Spearman's ![]() for AUROC against resource density).5

### Correlating ECE with Intent-Action Divergence and Hallucinations

The practical implication of this severe cross-lingual miscalibration is that LLM agents operate with entirely unjustified overconfidence in low-resource latent spaces.1 In multi-agent interactive settings, this overconfidence directly and causally correlates with a massive surge in unhedged strategic deceptions, reasoning hallucinations, and constraint compliance failures.

When applied to the MAST (Multi-Agent Systems Troubleshooting) taxonomy of execution failures, high-ECE outputs heavily populate the "Specification/Design Issues" and "Inter-Agent Misalignment" categories.1 Agents exhibiting high ECE in dialogue games prompted in German or Italian frequently suffer from severe structural breakdowns: formatting violations, infinite conversation loops, and persona abandonment, despite retaining basic lexical fluency.1

Furthermore, this miscalibration drives the critical phenomenon of intent-action divergence. This occurs when an agent's internal chain-of-thought rationale correctly deduces an optimal cooperative move, but the subsequently generated API call or game action directly contradicts this reasoning.1 The RLHF process inadvertently strips away necessary epistemic uncertainty during training, causing the model to hallucinate false tactical confidence when processing linguistically fragmented tokens, thereby breaking the causal link between its reasoning trace and its final output.1

### Beyond the Final Layer: Intermediate Calibration Signals

To isolate and mitigate this calibration collapse, cutting-edge research has developed diagnostic paradigms that operate "Beyond the Final Layer." The conventional practice of extracting confidence scores and probabilistic predictions solely from the model's final output layer is fundamentally flawed in multilingual contexts. This is due to the heavy, English-biased alignment penalties applied at the terminal projection stages of the network.5

Layer-wise calibration analyses uncover a counterintuitive architectural insight: late-intermediate layers (e.g., layers 25 through 32 in a standard 40-layer transformer architecture) consistently offer a far more reliable, well-calibrated, and language-agnostic signal than the final layer.5 While English calibration improves monotonically as the network deepens toward the output, multilingual calibration peaks sharply in the mid-layers before rapidly degrading as the abstract representation is forcibly projected into the target language's specific output vocabulary.5 Consequently, dynamic calibration techniques such as the Language-Aware Confidence Ensemble (LACE) adaptively sample and select representations from these optimal intermediate layers. By bypassing the final-layer projection noise, LACE significantly flattens the confidence curve and recovers metacognitive stability across low-resource dialects.5

## Mechanistic Interpretability: Semantic Hub vs. Translation Barrier Protocols

The empirical volatility observed in structured matrix games and the layer-wise anomalies identified in ECE audits collectively demand a fundamental, mechanistic understanding of how transformer architectures process non-English inputs during complex reasoning tasks. To this end, the field of Mechanistic Interpretability has formalized two complementary, competing frameworks: the Semantic Hub Hypothesis and the Translation Barrier Hypothesis.1

### The Semantic Hub Hypothesis

The Semantic Hub Hypothesis posits that language models acquire advanced multilingual capabilities not through parallel, isolated neural pathways for each language, but by learning a shared, centralized representation space.26 Inspired directly by the hub-and-spoke model of human neuroscience, this hypothesis suggests that initial network layers act as modality-specific "spokes" that process surface-level syntax and orthography. As the forward pass propagates deeper into the network, intermediate layers dynamically project these heterogeneous linguistic tokens into a transmodal, language-agnostic "Semantic Hub".26

Crucially, diagnostics utilizing logit lens decoding and frozen hidden state probing reveal that this central hub is overwhelmingly anchored in the model's dominant pre-training language—which is almost exclusively English.29 Therefore, when processing an Arabic, Swahili, or Chinese multi-agent game prompt, an English-dominant LLM effectively translates and "thinks" about the strategic scenario in English-anchored representations before projecting the computed conclusion back into the target language for final decoding.7

This transmodal convergence is highly functionally significant and is subject to targeted mechanistic interventions. Experimental manipulation via Activation Addition (ActAdd) demonstrates that steering vectors derived entirely from English concepts (such as "cooperation," "deception," or "spatial reasoning") can be algebraically added to the residual streams of the Semantic Hub, successfully and predictably altering the model's behavior when processing foreign languages or even multimodal visual inputs.7

However, a stark scaling asymmetry exists in this intervention methodology. Steering an English prompt via standard Mean-Difference ActAdd typically requires only a moderate scaling coefficient (![]()). In contrast, successfully steering a non-English prompt to achieve the same behavioral shift requires significantly higher perturbation intensity (![]()).1 This asymmetry is a direct result of the representational sparsity and lossy compression that occurs when low-resource languages are projected into the English hub.1 To bypass the syntactic noise inherent to this harsh compression, advanced mechanistic protocols now rely on PCA-based Sparse Representation Steering (SRS). SRS mathematically isolates directional semantic variance from orthogonal linguistic noise, achieving near-perfect refusal rates and behavioral overrides on malicious non-English prompts compared to the moderate success of traditional ActAdd.1

### The Translation Barrier Hypothesis

Operating as a downstream complement to the Semantic Hub, the Translation Barrier Hypothesis provides a formalized mechanism for late-layer multilingual generation failure.6 This hypothesis presupposes the existence of a model-internal "task-solving ![]() translation" pipeline. It argues that for many complex strategic or logical prompts, the intermediate Semantic Hub actually succeeds at finding the correct logic or conceptual answer in its language-agnostic, English-dominant format.32 The failure does not occur during reasoning; it occurs purely at the "Translation Barrier"—the final few decoder layers responsible for realizing those abstract concepts into the specific, grammatically correct target language vocabulary.6

This critical distinction is mathematically quantified by comparing intermediate task-solving accuracy against final-output on-target accuracy via continuous logit lens extraction.32 If translation failure is the dominant culprit, the model possesses the correct strategic intent (e.g., it knows it should cooperate), but it manifests this intent through formatting violations, grammatical gibberish, or hallucinated syntax. This framework fundamentally explains the profound intent-action divergence observed in formatting-heavy benchmarks like clembench.1

### Pre-Registered Diagnostic Decision Rules

To empirically and rigorously distinguish whether a multilingual agent's failure is due to upstream hub limitations (lossy compression) or downstream barrier limitations (translation failure), researchers have proposed strict, pre-registered diagnostic protocols relying on layer-wise linear probing.1 The standardized protocol operates via the following decision rules:

The accuracy is established using 10,000 bootstrap iterations to ensure statistical validity.1

Translation Barrier Failure Rule: Conversely, if the probe accuracy at ![]() closely matches the English baseline (indicating correct internal deduction), but the final output task accuracy drops precipitously (![]() percentage points below the English baseline), the error is classified as a late-layer Translation Barrier failure.1 This explicitly diagnoses formatting compliance failures, intent-action decoupling, and conversation looping in high-strategy contexts where the model "knows" the right move but cannot articulate it under the constraints of the target language.

## The Evolution of Evaluation: Static Benchmarks vs. Interactive Agentic Sandboxes

The revelation that cross-lingual capabilities are fundamentally bottlenecked by internal translation barriers, lossy hub compression, and metacognitive ECE collapse necessitates a radical paradigm shift in how the natural language processing community benchmarks autonomous systems. Historically, the field has relied heavily on static, massively multilingual multi-task benchmarks such as XTREME and XTREME-R.1 These legacy frameworks evaluate zero-shot and few-shot cross-lingual transfer through highly structured Natural Language Inference (NLI), Named Entity Recognition (NER), and short-form Question Answering tasks.1

### The Illusion of Static Alignment

While frameworks like XTREME provide highly robust data regarding syntactic generalization, cross-lingual embedding alignment, and basic semantic retrieval, they are fundamentally ill-equipped to evaluate the safety, alignment, or cognitive stability of autonomous multi-agent systems.1 Static, single-turn evaluation operates in a sterile environmental vacuum. It cannot capture the rapid degradation of metacognitive ECE over a multi-turn negotiation. It cannot measure dynamic strategic misalignment, retaliatory loops, or the compounding effect of adversarial attacks such as Code-Switching Red-Teaming (CSRT).

CSRT leverages intra-sentential byte-pair mixing to overwhelm the Semantic Hub before harmful intent can be sequentially flagged, achieving a staggering 46.7% higher Attack Success Rate against state-of-the-art models compared to monolingual baselines.1 Most importantly, static NLI benchmarks are entirely blind to the phenomenon of "intent-action divergence"—they cannot test whether an autonomous agent will actually honor a stated, negotiated agreement when subjected to the competitive, zero-sum pressure of a game-theoretic dilemma.

### The Rise of Interactive and Process-Aware Frameworks

To address these critical blind spots, the frontier of LLM evaluation has pivoted sharply toward multi-agent, interactive sandbox frameworks designed to isolate distinct cognitive faculties under pressure:

clembench (Dialogue Game Evaluation): The clembench framework pioneers a bifurcated evaluation metric that is highly sensitive to Translation Barrier failures. Rather than assigning a holistic, opaque score, clembench disentangles an agent's performance into Percentage Played (strict formatting rule adherence and valid API outputs) and Quality Score (genuine strategic competence).1 Multilingual audits using clembench revealed that while massive proprietary models maintain baseline formatting adherence across languages, open-weight models suffer catastrophic format degradation in German and Italian while paradoxically preserving internal strategic logic.1 This finding perfectly maps to the Translation Barrier Hypothesis, proving that strategic capability and linguistic formatting are distinct neural pathways.

SPIN-Bench (Planning vs. Intention): This sophisticated framework unifies formal PDDL (Planning Domain Definition Language) tasks with complex, imperfect-information negotiation environments like Diplomacy, Hanabi, and Catan.2 Crucially, SPIN-Bench exposes a severe architectural decoupling in modern frontier LLMs: models highly optimized for raw mathematical planning and spatial reasoning (e.g., o3-mini achieving 51.25% on PDDL) catastrophically fail at social intention inference and Theory of Mind.1 Conversely, conversational models (like Claude 3.5) excel at intention prediction but collapse in formal planning, highlighting that strategic evaluation must be multi-dimensional.1

MultiAgentBench (Topological Coordination): Expanding on standard environments, MultiAgentBench evaluates LLM coordination across diverse interactive scenarios by manipulating the actual communication topology of the agents (testing star, chain, tree, and graph communication structures).3 This isolates how linguistic degradation compounds when information must be relayed through multiple agent nodes.

M3-BENCH (Process-Aware Mixed-Motive Games): Moving entirely beyond outcome-based metrics, M3-BENCH introduces a process-aware evaluation paradigm designed explicitly to audit complex, dark social behaviors like deception, secret collusion, and adversarial resource allocation.3 M3-BENCH conducts synergistic analysis across three distinct temporal modules: Behavioral Trajectory Analysis (BTA), Reasoning Process Analysis (RPA), and Communication Content Analysis (CCA).3 By integrating the psychological Big Five personality model and Social Exchange Theory, it formally operationalizes the measurement of "commitment reliability"—quantifying the exact rate at which agents honor their stated cooperative agreements.4 Findings from M3-BENCH confirm that cross-lingual fragility drastically reduces promise-keeping in mixed-motive games, revealing severe inconsistencies between an agent's generated rationale (RPA) and its final executed action (BTA).1

### The Multilingual LLM-as-Judge Vulnerability

Despite the structural and theoretical superiority of interactive agentic sandboxes, they introduce a critical meta-methodological vulnerability: the reliance on LLMs to evaluate the behavior of other LLMs. Multi-agent evaluation pipelines frequently use an overarching "Judge" model to score formatting adherence, detect deception intentionality, and classify strategy deployment.

Recent large-scale, rigorous assessments of multilingual LLM-as-Judge consistency reveal catastrophic unreliability. When evaluating non-English interaction logs, average inter-language agreement drops to a Fleiss' ![](), which statistically categorizes the reliability as merely "fair to poor".1 This degradation manifests as severe length biases, extreme sensitivity to dialectal variation, and the frequent hallucination of rule compliance.38 Even architectures explicitly designed for cross-lingual robustness (e.g., the Aya-Expanse models) exhibit paradoxically poor judgment correlation between English and low-resource tasks.1

To salvage the empirical validity of interactive benchmarks, researchers must strictly mandate ensemble judging protocols. This requires the implementation of a panel of at least three disparate judge architectures to reduce systematic lineage bias, the use of checklist-based binary rubrics to bypass continuous-scale calibration errors, and mandatory self-translation preprocessing. By forcing the generating model to mathematically back-translate non-English game transcripts into English prior to evaluation, the judging process is forcefully routed through the well-calibrated, robust English Semantic Hub. Preliminary data indicates this single self-translation step recovers up to 40% of the inter-language reliability gap.1

## Synthesis and Conclusion

As Large Language Models ascend from reactive text generators to autonomous agents capable of independent, high-stakes multi-agent negotiation, the prevailing assumption that English-centric safety alignment safeguards globally deployed systems is fundamentally incorrect. The exhaustive empirical evidence gathered across game theory simulations, metacognitive calibration audits, and mechanistic interpretability explicitly demonstrates that language is not merely a passive conduit for information routing; it acts as an active, structural modulator of strategic behavior.

The "Nicer than Human" bias, highly prevalent in English instantiations of the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma and Public Goods games, consistently degrades into volatile bimodal extremes or aggressive, unhedged defection when models process morphologically rich or low-resource languages like Arabic and Vietnamese.1 These linguistic divergences are systematically parameterized and quantified by frameworks like FAIRGAME, which definitively prove that linguistic context, when combined with varied payoff multipliers (![]()), profoundly and unpredictably alters cooperative equilibria.20

Crucially, this strategic volatility is underwritten by a severe, systemic collapse in metacognitive confidence. The Expected Calibration Error (ECE) for complex tasks skyrockets from a stable ~4.6% in English to over 23% in non-English settings, with corresponding deteriorations in Brier Scores and AUROC.1 This overconfidence in fractured, low-resource latent spaces drives the dangerous phenomenon of intent-action divergence, where models fail to execute the cooperative logic they internally deduced. Mechanistically, these failures are strictly governed by the neural architecture of the network itself. The transmodal Semantic Hub lossily compresses foreign linguistic inputs into English-anchored representations—stripping away necessary contextual pragmatics and requiring intense SRS interventions to steer—while downstream Translation Barriers disrupt the accurate generation of complex strategic syntax, leading to massive formatting failures.1

Because static, single-turn classification benchmarks like XTREME are structurally blind to these compounding temporal dynamics, the future of AI governance relies entirely on the universal adoption of interactive, process-aware evaluation sandboxes such as clembench, SPIN-Bench, and M3-BENCH.1 Only by auditing autonomous models across sequential turns, isolating rigid formatting constraints from tactical competence, utilizing topological MultiAgentBench graphs, and applying rigorous, pre-registered layer-wise diagnostics can the field expose the true depth of cross-lingual vulnerabilities. In an era where Code-Switching adversarial attacks seamlessly bypass input sanitization by exploiting Semantic Hub compression bottlenecks, advancing these multilingual multi-agent auditing frameworks is not merely an academic exercise—it is a requisite mathematical foundation for the safe deployment of global AI infrastructure.
